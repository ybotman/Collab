# The Mission-Critical Lock-In Trust Narrative

**The canonical answer to Erik Dove's #1 fear.** Built on ratified invariant #9
("Customer co-holds the key," phased). This is the language Toby uses in the room —
and the language I (Rupert/HDTS) defend in the dual-advocate model.

---

## 1. The fear, named precisely

From the 2026-06-26 COO capture, Erik's #1 fear is **mission-critical hostage lock-in**:
if BHS routes load-bearing functions through HDTS —

- membership payments
- new-member acquisition
- starting a chapter
- music IP

— then a falling-out, a price hike, or an HDTS failure could **hold BHS hostage**. He is a CFO
(and acting COO). He is scanning for the trap. Treat the fear as legitimate, because it is.

## 2. Why the naïve pitch fails (and is dangerous)

The tempting pitch is the absolute: *"Don't worry — we cannot read your data."*

**Do not say this.** It is false today and it breaks on first contact:
- Toby (the HDTS HITM) is a hands-on participant in **every** project BHS runs with us.
- Reading client data is part of doing the work. So the key is — and must be — **co-held** today.
- If we pitch the absolute and Erik later learns Toby opened a file to do the job, **we have lied
  to a CFO about the one thing he was watching.** That is the betrayal he was scanning for, delivered
  by our own hand.

An honest, slightly-less-shiny claim beats a shiny claim that detonates later. Always.

## 3. The honest narrative — lock-in as a dial Erik controls

> **"You hold the key *with* us today, and you have a path to hold it *alone* as you mature."**

The three beats Toby says, in order:

1. **Co-custody today, and that's correct.** "Right now, Toby is in every project with you. Reading
   your data is how the work gets done — so the encryption key is co-held, by you and by us. That's
   not a loophole; it's an honest reflection of how we work together today."
2. **A real exit, on your timeline.** "As BHS grows into running more of this yourselves, you opt into
   **sole-custody** — you hold the key alone, and at that point we *cryptographically cannot* read
   your data, even if asked. That's a roadmap goal, ~a year out, on your maturity — not a someday."
3. **You control the dial.** "The lock-in you're worried about has a built-in exit, and **you** decide
   when to use it. Co-custody → sole-custody is your maturity decision, not our permission."

## 4. Why this is *stronger* than the absolute (the CFO logic)

- **It survives reality.** Nothing to walk back when Toby touches a project — because we already
  told him Toby would.
- **It reframes lock-in as a dial, not a trap.** The thing he fears becomes a control he holds.
- **It pairs comfort with capability.** The same sentence that reassures ("you hold it with us")
  also sells the roadmap ("you can hold it alone").
- **It's the no-Google / CMEK / HIPAA-path win at the same time** — invariant #9 is one principle
  with two doors (trust + security). Erik gets both in one answer.

## 5. Objection handling

| Erik might say… | Toby answers… |
|---|---|
| "So today you *can* read everything?" | "Where it's reasonably necessary to do the work, yes — and today it always is, because Toby's in every project. We're telling you that plainly. The alternative is a vendor who claims they can't and quietly does." |
| "What stops you reading it for the *wrong* reason?" | "Two things: it's co-held, so it's auditable; and the dual-advocate model — Clifton argues for BHS, Rupert argues for HDTS, and we won't build anything where the two don't agree." |
| "What if we want out entirely?" | "Sole-custody is the exit. You opt in, you hold the key alone, we cryptographically can't read it. Your data, your timeline." |
| "A year is a long time." | "It's a maturity goal, not a wall. The protections — co-custody, audit, dual-advocate — are live on day one. Sole-custody is the *additional* step you take when you're ready to run more yourself." |

## 6. How this rides the dual-advocate model

This narrative is defensible **in the open**, which is exactly why it fits the dual-advocate operating
model (client-blessed 2026-06-26):
- **Rupert (HDTS)** can argue it without hedging — it's true, so there's no exposure.
- **Clifton (BHS)** can accept it without risk — it's not a claim that embarrasses BHS later.
- It satisfies the standing rule: *"won't build anything where the two don't agree."* Co-custody with a
  documented exit is something both advocates can sign.
